Why this research matters

The vox pops reflect a widely held assumption. But our research found it's more complicated than that.

Most people, when asked, assume that democracies are better at tackling climate change. The logic makes intuitive sense: elected governments are accountable to citizens, and citizens increasingly demand climate action. But assumptions, however reasonable, need testing.

According to the V-Dem Institute's 2026 report, 74% of the world's population now lives under autocracy, with democratic indicators declining compared to the year 2000. At the same time, some of the world's most ambitious climate transitions are happening in countries that are not full democracies. So we started asking: is the relationship between democracy and climate performance as clear as people assume?

The answer, it turns out, is yes — but with outliers that force you to think harder about what is really driving climate action around the world. And that is exactly what this research set out to explore.

The research

61 countries. Three models. One clear pattern.

We set out to answer a question that sounds simple but turns out to be surprisingly hard to test: does the type of government a country has determine how seriously it takes addressing climate change?

To find out, we merged four publicly available datasets — the CCPI 2025, the EIU Democracy Index 2024, the ND-GAIN Vulnerability Index 2023, and World Bank GDP data — and analysed 61 countries using three separate regression models.

Each dot on this chart is a country. The x-axis is democratic governance. The y-axis is climate performance. Hover any dot to explore.
The finding

The trend line slants upward — clearly and consistently.

Countries that scored higher on democratic governance also tended to score higher on climate performance. Our bivariate regression confirmed it: a one-point increase on the Democracy Index predicts roughly 2.6 additional points on the CCPI.

To put that in concrete terms: a country moving from Algeria's democracy score to Portugal's — a four-point difference — predicts approximately 13 more points on the climate performance scale. That is the difference between ranking in the bottom third globally and ranking in the top quarter.

This finding does not dissolve when you account for other factors. Across all three models, the relationship between democracy and climate performance remained positive and statistically significant.
The surprise

Wealthier countries do not systematically outperform poorer ones.

GDP was consistently insignificant across all three of our models. Once democracy is already in the model, wealth adds nothing. This suggests that political will matters more than financial capacity when it comes to climate action.

But the analysis also produced a finding we did not fully anticipate. When we introduced climate vulnerability as a control variable — measuring exposure to drought, flooding, and sea-level rise — it emerged as a strong, independent predictor of climate performance.

In our full model, vulnerability carried a coefficient of 114, compared to 3.3 for democracy. Both effects are significant. Both matter.
The outliers

The implication? Urgency may substitute for accountability.

Countries that face more severe climate impacts tend to act more — regardless of their political system. This finding helps explain what is otherwise the most striking pattern in our data.

Morocco ranks 8th in the world for climate performance despite a Democracy Index of 4.97 — a constitutional monarchy with a parliamentary national legislative system, classified by the EIU as a hybrid regime. Pakistan similarly outperforms expectations. Meanwhile, the United States, a full democracy with a score of 7.85, ranks 57th. Canada ranks even lower.

Morocco and Pakistan face some of the highest climate vulnerability scores in our sample. The US and Canada face far less structural pressure to act — and our model shows it.
The underperformers

Democracy creates the conditions for climate action. It does not deliver it automatically.

Our findings are significant but not causal. This is a cross-sectional analysis — we capture a single point in time rather than tracking whether countries that become more democratic subsequently improve their climate performance.

The association between democracy and climate action is consistent and statistically significant across all our models. But association is not causation, and we cannot rule out other factors driving both.

What the data does make clear: the systems a country uses to govern itself are meaningfully associated with how seriously it responds to the climate crisis. And when those systems fall short, climate pressure itself appears to step in.
The norm

Denmark: what the expected pattern looks like.

Denmark ranks 4th in the world on climate performance with a CCPI score of 78.37 — and scores 9.28 on the Democracy Index, one of the highest in the world. It is the clearest example of what the data predicts: a well-functioning democracy translating public demand for climate action into actual policy.

Denmark has committed to a 70% emissions reduction by 2030, leads globally in offshore wind energy, and has consistently ranked at or near the top of the CCPI for over a decade. Its institutions create accountability — and its citizens use them.

The outlier

Morocco: performing like a democracy without being one.

Morocco ranks 5th globally on climate performance — ahead of France, Germany, and Sweden — with a CCPI of 68.32. A constitutional monarchy with a parliamentary national legislative system, it scores 4.97 on the Democracy Index, which the EIU classifies as a hybrid regime. By the logic of our main finding, it should not be performing this well.

But Morocco is one of the most climate-vulnerable countries in our dataset. It faces acute water scarcity, desertification, and extreme heat. The climate crisis is not a future scenario for Morocco — it is a present and structural reality that has driven significant renewable energy investment, particularly in solar power.

Morocco's Noor Ouarzazate solar complex is one of the largest in the world. Vulnerability, it seems, can drive ambition that democracy alone does not always guarantee.

What this means

Conclusion: systems matter — but so does pressure.

Our data tells a nuanced story. Democracy matters for climate action — but it is not the whole story. The political system a country has shapes its capacity and incentive to respond to the climate crisis. But real-world climate pressure — the lived experience of drought, flooding, and heat — can motivate action even in countries where democratic accountability is weak.

The finding that should concern citizens of wealthy democracies most is not Morocco's overperformance. It is the consistent underperformance of the United States, Canada, Australia, and Japan. These are countries with the institutions, the resources, and the democratic mandates to lead on climate. The data suggests they are choosing not to.

Democracy, our analysis suggests, is a necessary but not sufficient condition for serious climate action. What fills the gap between having democratic institutions and actually using them to protect the climate is a question our data cannot answer — but one that citizens in every democracy should be asking.

Methodology

How we did this

We merged four publicly available datasets and ran three OLS regression models — bivariate, multivariate without region, and multivariate with region (Africa as reference category) — to isolate the effect of democratic governance on climate performance.
