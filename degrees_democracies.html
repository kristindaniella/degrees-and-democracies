Vox Pops · What people think

"Do you think democracies are better at addressing climate change?"

Why this research matters

Democracy is often seen as a key driver of climate action. The data tells a more complex story.

Many of the responses point in the same direction: democratic governments are expected to be more accountable, more transparent, and more responsive to public pressure. The idea makes intuitive sense.

But when we began analysing climate performance across countries, we found that while democracies do tend to perform better on average, they do not explain the full picture. Some countries perform better than expected, while others fall behind. These outliers are not just exceptions — they point to additional forces shaping climate action.

"By combining data on climate performance, political systems, and climate vulnerability, we examine not only whether democracy matters — but what other forces may influence how countries respond to climate change."

— Research overview

This is where we focus our analysis. Understanding these dynamics helps move beyond simplified assumptions and gives a clearer picture of what drives real-world climate action.

The research

61 countries. Three models. One clear pattern.

We set out to answer a question that sounds simple but turns out to be surprisingly hard to test: does the type of government a country has determine how seriously it addresses climate change?

To find out, we merged four publicly available datasets — the CCPI 2025, the EIU Democracy Index 2024, the ND-GAIN Vulnerability Index 2023, and World Bank GDP data — and analysed 61 countries using three separate regression models.

Each dot on this chart is a country. The x-axis is democratic governance. The y-axis is climate performance. Hover any dot to explore.
The finding

The trend line slants upward — clearly and consistently.

Countries that scored higher on democratic governance also tended to score higher on climate performance. Our bivariate regression confirmed it: a one-point increase on the Democracy Index predicts roughly 2.6 additional points on the CCPI.

To put that in concrete terms: a country moving from Algeria's democracy score to Portugal's — a four-point difference — predicts approximately 13 more points on the climate performance scale.

This finding does not dissolve when you account for other factors. Across all three models, the relationship remained positive and statistically significant.
The surprise

Wealthier countries do not systematically outperform poorer ones.

GDP was consistently insignificant across all three of our models. Once democracy is already in the model, wealth adds nothing. This suggests that political will matters more than financial capacity when it comes to climate action.

But the analysis also produced a finding we did not fully anticipate. When we introduced climate vulnerability as a control variable — measuring exposure to drought, flooding, and sea-level rise — it emerged as a strong, independent predictor.

In our full model, vulnerability carried a coefficient of 114, compared to 3.3 for democracy. Both effects are significant. Both matter.
The outliers

Urgency may substitute for accountability.

Countries that face more severe climate impacts tend to act more — regardless of their political system. Morocco ranks 8th in the world for climate performance despite a Democracy Index of 4.97, classified as a hybrid regime. Pakistan similarly outperforms expectations.

Meanwhile, the United States — a full democracy scoring 7.85 — ranks 57th. Canada ranks even lower.

Morocco and Pakistan face some of the highest climate vulnerability scores in our sample. The US and Canada face far less structural pressure to act — and our model shows it.
The caveat

Democracy creates conditions for climate action. It does not deliver it automatically.

Our findings are significant but not causal. This is a cross-sectional analysis — we capture a single point in time rather than tracking whether countries that become more democratic subsequently improve their climate performance.

The association is consistent and statistically significant across all our models. But association is not causation.

What the data does make clear: the systems a country uses to govern itself are meaningfully associated with how seriously it responds to the climate crisis. And when those systems fall short, climate pressure itself appears to step in.
The norm

Denmark: what the expected pattern looks like.

Denmark ranks 4th in the world on climate performance with a CCPI score of 78.37 — and scores 9.28 on the Democracy Index, one of the highest globally. It is the clearest example of what the data predicts: a well-functioning democracy translating public demand for climate action into actual policy.

Denmark has committed to a 70% emissions reduction by 2030 and leads globally in offshore wind. Its institutions create accountability — and its citizens use them.

The outlier

Morocco: performing like a democracy without being one.

Morocco ranks 5th globally on climate performance — ahead of France, Germany, and Sweden — with a CCPI of 68.32. By the logic of our main finding, it should not be performing this well.

But Morocco faces acute water scarcity, desertification, and extreme heat. The climate crisis is not a future scenario for Morocco — it is a present and structural reality.

Morocco's Noor Ouarzazate solar complex is one of the largest in the world. Vulnerability, it seems, can drive ambition that democracy alone does not always guarantee.

Conclusion

Systems matter — but so does pressure.

What the data does make clear is that the systems a country uses to govern itself are meaningfully associated with how seriously it responds to the climate crisis. Democracy — with its mechanisms for accountability, transparency, and public pressure — tends to produce better climate outcomes on average.

But the outliers force a harder question. When democratic systems fall short, when fossil fuel economies and low climate vulnerability reduce the pressure to act, the expected advantage disappears. And when authoritarian or hybrid governments face existential climate risk, urgency can drive ambition that democracy alone does not guarantee.

Morocco and Pakistan are not just curiosities in the data. They are a signal: that what drives climate action may be less about the form of government, and more about whether a country has something to lose.

Methodology

How this research was conducted

This is an observational, cross-sectional study. We cannot establish causality from this data.

Dataset Coverage What it measures
CCPI 202561 countriesClimate Change Performance Index — energy, renewables, efficiency, policy
EIU Democracy Index 2024167 countriesElectoral process, functioning government, political culture, civil liberties
ND-GAIN 2023180+ countriesClimate vulnerability: exposure to drought, flooding, food security, sea level
World Bank GDP 2024GlobalGDP per capita (current USD) — economic capacity control variable

Three OLS regression models were run: bivariate (democracy → CCPI), with GDP and region controls, and a full model including climate vulnerability. Country name mismatches were reconciled manually before merging.
